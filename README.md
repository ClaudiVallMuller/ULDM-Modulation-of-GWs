### Summary of the work


One of the major open problems in astrophysics is the problem of _dark matter_: 85% of the total mass of the universe is unknown. Among the several candidates for this unseen mass are _ultralight bosons_, incredibly light particles that do not interact significantly with the other known particles. An optimistic path to probe this candidate is the well-established mechanism of _black hole superradiance_: if ultralight bosons exist, dense clouds of these particles are expected to grow around rotating black holes by extracting their rotational energies. 

How can we potentially detect these clouds of ultralight particles? One option is to study the effect that they may have on _gravitational waves_, waves of gravity that were first observed in 2015 and that the next generation of detectors (~2030s) will be able to detect with high precision. If a source of these gravitational waves is located within a superradiant cloud, and the cloud is in a particular configuration where two of its modes coexist, then we expect the gravitational wave to be perturbed with respect to the case where the cloud was not there. This perturbation takes the form of a _frequency modulation_, a change in the frequency of the wave that depends periodically on time (see Figure 1.1). 

The two research questions that this work studied are the following:

**(1) What models of ultralight boson clouds can generate states where the two lowest-energetic modes coexist?**

   I found that _real scalar fields_ do not generate such states, while _complex scalar fields_ and _self-interacting fields_ do allow these states of coexistence to happen in astrophysical timescales. 
   The results are shown in Figure 3.1 (real scalar field), Figure 4.2 (complex scalar field) and Figure 5.1 (self-interacting scalar field). 
          
**(2) Is the frequency modulation effect within the reach of the new generation of gravitational wave detectors, such as the Einstein Telescope?**

   I found that a first simple calculation of detectability prospects indicates that the Einstein Telescope may be able to probe ultralight bosons with masses of $\sim 10^{-15} - 10^{-14}$ eV, while DECIGO may probe masses of $\sim 10^{-18} - 10^{-16}$ eV. The results are shown in Figure 6.4 (complex scalar field, ET), and Figure 6.5 (self-interacting field, DECIGO). 
   
   This is a first approximation to a proper detectability prospect, and more detailed Fisher matrix or Bayesian approaches are needed to confirm this detectability power.  


### Brief information about the codes and figures
The number 1-6 labels each code and the Figures generated by it.

**1. Perturbed gravitational wave**

   Computes the frequency modulation effect of a superradiant cloud on a realistic gravitational wave.
   
   _Figure 1.1_: Comparison between the strain $h(t)$ of a perturbed gravitational wave against an unperturbed one. The perturbation and the wave contain all the physics of the problem, but the perturbation is exaggerated by a factor of $\sim 1000$.
   
   _Figure 1.2_: Difference between the phase in Fourier space of the two waveforms of Figure 1.1. This quantity is key for the data analysis of gravitational waves.

**2. Initial field configuration**

   Computes the initial occupation numbers of the cloud, assuming that the dark matter halo is the seed for superradiance. 
   
   Figure 2.1: Comparison between the density profile generated by the cloud and an NFW profile. Shows that occupation numbers of $N \sim 10^{77}$ are necessary to match the NFW profile with the cloud.
   
   Figure 2.2: Same plot as Figure 2.1, but with the proper computation of the initial occupation numbers used in the work. Shows that our computation matches correctly the NFW profile.

**3. Real scalar field - cloud dynamics**

   Computes the timescales of growth and decay of the two relevant modes of the cloud, for the case that the scalar field is free and real. 
   
   Figure 3.1: Timescales of growth of the 211 and 322 modes, compared against the timescale of decay of the 211 and the age of the universe $H_0^{-1}$.
   
   Figure 3.2: Necessary conditions to have coexistence between the two modes. The allowed region needs to be above the green line and below the orange one: there is no coexistence in this model.

**4. Complex scalar field - cloud dynamics**

   Solves numerically the dynamics of the cloud (system of ODEs) when there is no decay to gravitational waves (free complex scalar field). Computes the region of parameter space where coexistence is allowed.
   
   Figure 4.1: Numerical solution of the dynamics of the two relevant modes of the cloud as function of time. Shows that there is coexistence in the transition between 211 and 322 domination.
   
   Figure 4.2: Region of parameter space (mass of the central black hole, cloud constant $\alpha$) where coexistence between the two modes is realistic (forms in the age of the universe, and lasts for $10^6$ years)

**5. Self-interacting field - cloud dynamics**

   Computes the region of parameter space where coexistence is allowed. Uses expressions from the literature to compute the region.
   
   Figure 5.1: Region of parameter space (mass of the ultralight boson, strength of self-interactions) where coexistence between the two modes is realistic. Same conditions as Figure 4.2. We use $M = 10^{6} M_\odot$ for the mass of the black hole in the center of the cloud.

**6. SNR detectability**

   Computes the region of parameter space where the next generation of GW detectors may be sensitive, using a simple signal-to-noise ratio $\text{SNR}_\delta > 1$ condition from the literature.
   
   Figure 6.1: A necessary condition for detection is that the timescale of modulation is smaller than the time of observation of a GW signal. The shaded areas are the regions of parameter space (mass of the central black hole, cloud constant $\alpha$) where this condition is satisfied, for different fiducial values of observation times.
   
   Figure 6.2: Comparison between the coupling strength of different effects contributing to the frequency modulation of GWs. The condition of the Doppler term (blue line) to be larger than the first post-Newtonian term (orange line) sets a minimum value of $\Lambda$.
   
   Figure 6.3: Region of parameter space, for a complex scalar field, where the coexistence state of Figure 4.2 satisfies the detectability condition. The region where the condition of Figure 6.1 is satisfied is below the green line. We use a BBH source of GWs detected by ET.
   
   Figure 6.4: Same plot as Figure 6.3, but using the mass of the ultralight boson as x-axis instead of the cloud constant $\alpha$. Shows that may probe a range of $\mu \sim 10^{-15} - 10^{-14}$ eV. The condition of Figure 6.1 is satisfied for the region above the green line.
   
   Figure 6.5: Region of parameter space, for a self-interacting field, where the coexistence state of Figure 5.1 satisfies the detectability condition. The region shaded in violet does not satisfy the condition of Figure 6.1. We use a BBH source of GWs detected by DECIGO. Repeating the same plot for masses different from the used $M = 10^6 M_\odot$ gives a probing range of $\mu \sim 10^{-18} - 10^{-15}$ eV.
