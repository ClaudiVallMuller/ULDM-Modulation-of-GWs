### Summary of the work


One of the major open problems in astrophysics is the problem of _dark matter_: an 85% of the total mass of the universe is unknown. Among the several candidates for this unseen mass are _ultralight bosons_, incredibly light particles that do not interact significantly with the other known particles. An optimistic path to probe this candidate is the well-established mechanism of _black hole superradiance_: if ultralight bosons exist, dense clouds of these particles are expected to grow around rotating black holes by extracting its rotational energy. 

How can we potentially detect these clouds of ultralight particles? One option is to study the effect that they may have on _gravitational waves_, waves of gravity that were first observed in 2015 and that the next generation of detectors (~2030s) will be able to detect with high precision. If a source of these gravitational waves is located within a superradiant cloud, and the cloud exist in a particular configuration where two of its "modes" coexist, then we expect the gravitational wave to be perturbed with respect to the case where the cloud was not there. This perturbation takes the form of a _frequency modulation_, a change in the frequency of the wave that depends periodically on time (see Figure 1). 

The two research questions that this work studied and found first answers are the following:

(1) What models of ultralight boson clouds can generate states where the two lowest-energetic "modes" coexist?

   I found that _real scalar fields_ do not generate such states, while _complex scalar fields_ and _self-interacting fields_ do allow these states of coexistence to happen in astrophysical timescales. 
   The results are shown in Figures YYYY
          
(2) Is the frequency modulation effect within the reach of the new generation of gravitational wave detectors, such as the Einstein Telescope?

   I found that a first simple calculation of detectability prospects indicates that the Einstein Telescope may be able to probe ultralight bosons with masses of $\sim 10^{-15} - 10^{-14}$ eV, while DECIGO may probe masses of $\sim 10^{-18} - 10^{-16}$ eV. This is a first approximation to a proper detectability prospect, and more detailed Fisher matrix or Bayesian approaches are needed to confirm this detectability power.
